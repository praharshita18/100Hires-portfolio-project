## Post 1

People overcomplicate positioning.
There are really just three strategies...

Strategy 1: Position in a mature category against existing vendors

You place yourself in an established category and argue why you're better than the competition.

The upside:
Customers already have budget for it, actively search for it, and don't need educating.

The risk:
Mature categories have big, entrenched players. Without serious differentiation and resources, you'll struggle to make the shortlist.

Choose this if...
• the category is large enough to meet your growth goals
• you have the resources to compete head-to-head for the whole market
• OR you're willing to focus an underserved subsegment

Strategy 2: Take an existing (but small) category to a new audience

You position in a known (but likely nascent) category and sell it to people who haven't heard of it yet.

(E.g. Skrillex bringing Dubstep to the mainstream. DocuSign didn't invent e-signature, but they focused on selling it to companies that used paper. Dropbox didn't invent file sharing, but they popularized it.)

The upside:
• Less direct competition, more greenfield opportunity

The risk:
• You have to educate the market rather than waiting for inbound demand
• You also inherit the category's reputation (for good or bad)

Choose this if...
• you believe the category will grow and you can become a leader as it solidifies
• you have the marketing patience to play the long game

Strategy 3: Create your own category

You refuse to fit into anyone else's box and seek to build a new one.

The upside:
You get full control to define both the problem and solution framing.

The risks:
• you need to pick the right-sized problem (too broad and it's unbelievable)
• first movers rarely win (Airbnb beat Vrbo, Google beat AltaVista), and large incumbents may simply copy your blueprint once you've proven the market, as Microsoft did to Slack.

Choose this if...
• you genuinely believe any existing category label would hurt more than help
• you're prepared to invest years building awareness from scratch

## Post 2

"The CEO has the final say, so we should write the homepage for them."

I can't tell you how many times I've heard this from founders.

Usually, they've had a deal that died because the CEO (or another high-level decision maker) killed it.

To lower their chances of this happening again, they want to make sure the homepage speaks to that decision maker.

And so they take out all the product's features and differentiation and replace it with tons of "outcomes."

→ "boost your ROI"
→ "increase your revenue"
→ "modernize your business"

Just one problem...
the executive will never read your homepage

The CEO is too busy to be shopping for software, so they will pass this responsibility onto someone lower level.

This person (known as the Champion) is trying to understand why they should pick this platform over the alternatives.

You know what DOESN'T help them make that decision?

→ seeing that the product will "boost their ROI," "increase their revenue," or "modernize their business"

This is what EVERY company says.

What WOULD help them make the decision?

Actually explaining HOW your product solves their problem → especially compared to the alternative solutions.

The main takeaway:
• write your homepage for the champion, not the final decision maker

**There is one exception**

If you're targeting small companies where the CEO/executive is still very much involved in the day-to-day, they may actually be the one visiting your page.

If this is the case, they ALSO will be trying to answer the same questions as the Champion — i.e. why choose you over other solutions.

So you should still explain how the product works and solves their problem.

## Post 3

I'm not saying all marketers are liars,
but... 😬 😬 😬

A LOT of what we do is promise things that go far beyond what we can guarantee.

We've been trained to "sell the outcome."

And in business contexts that means selling the BUSINESS outcome.

Things like "getting more customers," "increasing revenue," "business growth," etc.

Here's the problem:
business outcomes are never driven by one thing

Every BUSINESS outcome is determined by an interlocking set of factors that span the entire BUSINESS.

Whether or not a B2B software company "wins more customers" is a function of...

• Precise ICP targeting and segmentation
• Marketing reach within target roles and industries
• Executive thought leadership and content
• Case studies, logos, and peer validation
• Brand credibility
• Differentiation from legacy tools or point solutions
• Positioning [WHAT WE DO]
• Competitive landscape
• Free trials, pilot programs, or POCs
• Pricing and contract flexibility
• Sales execution and account-based strategy
• Tech stack & ecosystem fit
• Regulatory and compliance readiness (SOC 2, HIPAA)
• Procurement process alignment
• Product breadth and roadmap confidence
• Channel or partner integrations
• Market timing and budget cycles
• Platform stability & uptime
• User experience
• Implementation onboarding support

And on and on and on.

No product/service impacts all these factors.

And so... no product can guarantee a business-level outcome.

To tell someone that adopting a CRM will make them "win more customers" is simply a lie.

They will win more customers provided that...

ALL THOSE OTHER FACTORS THAT ARE UNRELATED TO A CRM ARE WORKING PROPERLY.

If you take someone who is just starting out and has no product yet and they buy a CRM...

...they will not "win more customers."

So what are TRUE outcomes that CRMs can guarantee?

They can help teams be more organized.
They can help teams do forecasting.
They can help teams see all customer touchpoints.

These are the REAL reasons customers purchase CRMs.

Notice that these outcomes are very "functional."

This is because all B2B software is, in essence, "workflow software."

Workers get software to help them do their work
(hence it's "workflow software")

A rule of thumb...

if you would feel very uncomfortable putting "increase revenue" in your contract as a guaranteed outcome...

...maybe avoid saying it in your marketing materials.

I understand you might think I am a hack for saying this.

But my co-founder and I do roughly $1.7m a year as a three-person consultancy, and we have never once promised any of these business outcomes.

We ONLY promise what we can GUARANTEE:
→ a clarified positioning strategy documented in a crystal clear homepage

If everything else is working,
then you WILL get those good business outcomes.

But it won't be as a result of us:
it will be the result of us + the thousands of other things you're doing that we won't touch.

## Post 4

Look at your homepage and see how YOU answer this question:
"what is your product?"

Do you at least say what product category you're in?
Or do you at least list the main use case of your product?

And are both of these specific enough that your ICP would immediately "get it"?

It may surprise you to find how few companies actually pass this simple test.

You will find far more answering the way the gigachads answer in the right side of the diagram:

1) outcomes-only
2) vision-only

Many will attempt the left side, but will make a category name that no one has ever heard of (i.e. "intelligent agreement management") which is the same as not saying any category at all.

Or they will pick a product use case that is so broad it doesn't actually tell us anything concrete:

"Our product is for marketing!"

If you don't adequately answer "what is it," then no one will care about anything else you have to say.

## Post 5

I ranked five Y Combinator startup homepages
(Spoiler: they are not great 🥴)

My criteria with each was simple:
- Is it clear what the product is and does?
- Is it clear what the product should be compared to?
- Is it clear why the product is better than its alternative?

Then in a very vibe-based way, I translated the answers to those questions (or lack thereof) to a score out of 5.

1) Paloma — 2/5

I could not find this company's LinkedIn page, further cementing my guess that they are not relying on marketing whatsoever to gain new business (and instead relying on personal network/intros, etc.)

It is nearly impossible to market "custom software development for anyone," so you're left growing the business via people you know personally. Not the most scalable method (unless you're Peter Thiel and your company is Palantir 🤷🏻‍♂️)

2) Uplift AI — 4.5/5

Honestly, I could have given them 5/5. Clear, articulate, differentiated strategy through and through.

3) Burnt — 3/5

This company has such an amazing opportunity to create an incredibly differentiated positioning, but instead they are resorting to the most generic pitch possible (things like "eliminate manual work and scale without adding headcount.").

Two ways to make this INSTANTLY 10x better would be to either say "why our AI agents are better than the other off-the-shelf generic ones" (and they have a really good specialization to make this argument just SING).

Or secondly, they could assume that their audience really does not know the first thing about AI agents, and then they could lead with extremely tangible ways the agents could improve these food distributors' lives.

A few use cases would make a WORLD of a difference.

4) Avent (YC S25) — 3/5

The same exact improvements could be made for Avent that I suggested for Burnt.

For people with low knowledge of AI agents, their arguments are not nearly specific or tangible enough to make it clear how their agents would improve their lives.

For people with high knowledge of AI agents, their arguments are not differentiated enough of what makes them different or better than the off the shelf ones from the big players.

5) Outrove (YC S25) — 3/5

I'm guessing this company got the (BAD!) advice that "nobody cares about your product! Just talk about outcomes you drive!"

However, they are in a space where there are MANY products that people use every day.

And so they need to immediately explain at a capability level how their product is different — not just in the outcomes, but in the MECHANISM.

Because when you read their homepage, it is not clear what product they would replace or even if they are doing anything different or new.

Since they are backed by Y Combinator, I think I can assume they ARE doing something very unique and different. But you'd just be hard pressed to find what that is with the current page!

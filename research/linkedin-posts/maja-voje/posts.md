## Post 1 — The GTM strategy checklist

> Before launching anything in B2B SaaS, I check these 10 boxes:
>
> ☐ ICP defined (not "SMBs" — specific job title, company size, trigger)
> ☐ Primary pain articulated in customer language (not our language)
> ☐ Category defined (are we creating or entering one?)
> ☐ Differentiation named (vs. the 2-3 alternatives buyers actually consider)
> ☐ Pricing logic validated with 3+ customer conversations
> ☐ Launch channel selected (1, not 5)
> ☐ First 100 customers identified by name
> ☐ Success metric for launch defined (not "awareness")
> ☐ Post-launch nurture sequence ready
> ☐ Win/loss review process defined
>
> If you can't check 8/10, you're not ready to launch.
> Delay the launch. Do the work.

## Post 2 — Positioning for new categories

> The hardest GTM challenge: launching a product in a category that doesn't exist yet.
>
> If you're creating a new category, you can't position against competitors.
> There aren't any yet.
>
> So you position against the status quo.
>
> Status quo = what buyers are doing today instead of using your product.
> Usually: spreadsheets, manual processes, cobbled-together solutions, doing nothing.
>
> Your positioning becomes:
> "If you're currently doing [status quo], there's a better way."
>
> The job of your content is to:
> 1. Make the problem undeniable
> 2. Make the status quo painful
> 3. Make your category feel inevitable
>
> In that order.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** Category creation GTM strategy  
**Approx. engagement:** 1,900+ likes  
**Why included:** Sophisticated positioning framework for category creators — highly relevant to B2B SaaS

## Post 3 — The growth loop model for B2B SaaS

> Most B2B SaaS growth strategies are linear:
> Acquire → Activate → Retain → Expand
>
> The best ones are loops:
> Acquire → Activate → Retain → Expand → Generate referrals → Acquire more
>
> The difference:
> Linear = each customer costs the same to acquire
> Loop = each happy customer makes the next one cheaper
>
> Examples of B2B SaaS growth loops:
> - Viral loop: Loom videos have Loom branding → viewers become users
> - Content loop: Content ranks → attracts ICPs → ICPs share content → more ICPs
> - Community loop: Users join community → contribute → community attracts more users
>
> Question to ask: what happens AFTER a customer is happy?
> If the answer is "nothing," you don't have a loop.

**Format:** Text with contrast and examples  
**Theme:** Growth loops, viral mechanics  
**Approx. engagement:** 2,400+ likes  
**Why included:** Growth loop framework is a distinctive Maja topic; directly actionable for B2B SaaS product teams

## Post 4 — Sequencing GTM channels

> The biggest GTM mistake I see: trying to be everywhere at once.
>
> Stage 1 (0-10 customers): Founder sells manually. No marketing. Direct outreach only.
>
> Stage 2 (10-100 customers): 1 channel. Pick the one where your ICP already has attention.
>
> Stage 3 (100-1000 customers): Add a second channel. Optimize the first.
>
> Stage 4 (1000+ customers): Add channels systematically. Only when existing ones are saturated.
>
> The rule: don't add a new channel until the previous one is working reliably.
>
> "Working reliably" = predictable CAC, repeatable process, someone who owns it.
>
> Channel discipline is a competitive advantage.
> Everyone wants to do all channels at once.
> Very few do one channel exceptionally.

**Format:** Staged list  
**Theme:** GTM channel sequencing  
**Approx. engagement:** 2,200+ likes  
**Why included:** Clear stage-based channel strategy; counters the common "be everywhere" mistake

## Post 5 — On the difference between tactics and strategy

> A tactic: "Post on LinkedIn every day."
>
> A strategy: "Build a LinkedIn presence that positions [founder] as the definitive voice on [category], so that when our ICP is ready to buy, we're the first brand they think of."
>
> Most B2B companies have tactics.
> Very few have strategies.
>
> The difference:
> A tactic tells you what to do.
> A strategy tells you what to optimize for, and how to know if you're winning.
>
> Without a strategy:
> - You can't say no to anything (every tactic sounds reasonable)
> - You can't measure success (engagement? clicks? revenue?)
> - You can't prioritize (everything feels equally important)
>
> Write the strategy first. The tactics follow.

**Theme:** Strategy vs. tactics distinction

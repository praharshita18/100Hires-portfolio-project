## Post 1

Snowflake's CMO doesn't open a dashboard in the morning.

Denise Persson runs marketing for 700 people. She's accountable for new-business pipeline. And she told us at SaaStr AI that the dashboard is basically dead.

Why? Dashboards only ever answered "what happened." Never "why." So you'd ping someone. Schedule a meeting. Sit with sales and argue about what the numbers meant.

Now she just asks her data directly, in plain English, and gets the answer plus a recommendation back in real time.

"Nobody gets Slack messages from me anymore."

→ 30% lower cost per opportunity in six months. Pulled fragmented media into one place, let the system optimize daily instead of waiting for a campaign to end and learning it failed.

→ The sales-marketing data war is over. One source of truth means no more fighting over whose dashboard is right before you discuss the actual business.

→ Bad data plus AI doesn't give you bad decisions. It gives you bad decisions faster and at scale. The agent amplifies whatever you feed it.

→ The budget mandate: deliver 40-50% growth with flat or fewer resources. Her words: ask for more headcount in 2026 and leadership looks at you like you don't understand where the company is.

→ The hiring profile flipped. The old spec was a list of certifications. Now it's adaptability, curiosity, change management. GTM engineer is the role they hire for.

The part most teams underestimate: building AI fluency across 700 people was her single biggest investment of the year. And the top of her usage leaderboard wasn't who she expected. It was the brand team.

She was also honest about what's not working yet. The leaderboard rewards usage, not outcomes, so she has to caveat it every month. "Build everything" is creating sprawl they'll have to rein in. Unlimited AI spend got adoption and a bill they'll reckon with in 2026.

## Post 2

We spend 5x more on the AI agents that run on top of our CRM than on our CRM itself.

Our Salesforce contract is real money. But our agent stack … Artisan (AI SDR), Qualified (inbound BDR), Agentforce, Momentum, Monaco, plus a bit to run Qbee and 10K .. that's where the serious budget goes.

The CRM is now infrastructure. The agents are the product.

Side note: we're basically ending use of Marketo. For years it functioned almost like a secondary CRM. But its API is so dated our agents can't work with it. If your agents can't read from it and write to it cleanly, it doesn't matter how much history lives inside it. It becomes a data silo.

A lot of tools that were mission-critical five years ago are going to get quietly deprecated over the next 18 months. Not because they got worse, but because they never updated their APIs for an agent first world.

Agent compatibility is the new integration checklist.

## Post 4

When growth slows ... is it sales + marketing's fault, or the product's fault?

This has led to office fights, drama, accusations, finger pointing, and more since the dawn of B2B software.

But the answer is different today. Why?

Because until the past 18 months or so, until the rise of agentic products, B2B software mostly was the same for years on end.

You'd add features, fix debt, catch up to competitors, and every few years, add a new product.

But it was mostly static. And so sales and marketing's goal was to jam more and more folks into the funnel, and get more and more of them to buy.

Today, products are incredibly fluid, and get stale incredibly quickly. You can't go 3-5 years between major releases.

Claude itself is literally 100x better than 12 months ago.

And so, while some of the "fault" may still be in sales and marketing, more likely it's a product problem today.

The pace of change, improvement, competition and more is like nothing we've seen before. Fire your VP of Sales if you want. But if your product isn't as competitive as it was 6-18 months ago, I doubt it will help.

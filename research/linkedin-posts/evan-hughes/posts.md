## Post 1

The biggest gap I see right now in marketing talent is critical thinking.

Not AI usage. Not prompting. Not tooling.

Just the ability to think critically and form your own point of view.

AI is making it really easy to produce more work faster. More copy. More strategy docs. More campaign ideas. More research summaries.

But I'm noticing a lot of marketers struggle once they have to evaluate the output.

Is this insight actually good?
Does this strategy make sense for the business?
Would a buyer care about this?
Do I care about this?
What's missing?
What assumptions are being made here?
What would make this fail?

This skill and muscle matters wayyyyy more now.

A couple things I'd recommend if you want to stay ahead of it:

Spend more time challenging ideas.

Including your own.

Most people stop at the first reasonable answer. Push further. Pressure test things. Ask better follow-up questions. Try to break the logic before someone else does.

Stop over-relying on frameworks and templates.

A framework should help organize thinking, not replace it. A lot of marketers know how to repeat strategy language without actually understanding the business problem underneath it.

The talent pool is shrinking for marketers who can take messy information, conflicting opinions, unclear data and still make smart decisions.

That's who I want on my team.

## Post 2

If I wanted a VP of Marketing job right now, I'd approach the process differently than most.

I wouldn't spend time proving I've done it before. I'd focus on showing I can do it for them now.

That's the shift most senior marketers miss. They talk in the past tense. "I built," "I led," "I scaled." None of that matters if you can't connect it to how you'll move their business forward today.

When I think about interviews at that level, these are my non-negotiables.

⸻

1. Partnership over politics
I ask how the CEO wants to be challenged.
If they don't, I'm out.
I'm not signing up to take orders. I'm looking to build a partnership where tension creates better ideas, not fear.

⸻

2. Data and judgment, not just dashboards
I ask how they define "data-driven."
If the answer is charts, not context, it's a red flag.
The best marketing leaders know numbers only matter if they inform judgment. That's the skill that compounds.

⸻

3. Brand isn't fluff
I ask how brand shows up in board conversations.
If it doesn't, marketing's already boxed in.
You can't lead demand strategy if brand is treated like design work.

⸻

4. Patience for compounding
I ask about their appetite for long-term marketing bets.
Most companies say they want growth, but what they really want is relief.
Relief from pressure. Relief from board expectations.
Compounding only works when you stop chasing quick wins and start building enduring ones.

⸻

5. Shared accountability
I ask how marketing, sales, and finance align on goals.
If I hear, "We have weekly syncs," I push further.
Alignment isn't meetings. It's shared math.
If the math isn't shared, the incentives aren't either.

⸻

When you've been in this game long enough, you start to see interviews less like a test and more like a mirror.

You're not trying to impress. You're trying to understand what you'd be walking into.

Can you lead? Can you challenge? Can you build something that compounds?

Those are the questions that matter.

Everything else is corporate meh on a resume.

## Post 4

Story time, about Refine Labs.

6-9 months ago we started investing heavily in building AI prompt libraries for ChatGPT.
At the time it felt pretty damn good. Revolutionary even. Standardize the prompts, build the workflows, create the templates, give people the playbook and everyone moves faster.

For a bit, it worked. Then the models changed.

What worked well in December suddenly wasn't the best way to do things anymore. New models went live. Reasoning got better. Context windows got bigger. Workflows changed. Things we thought we'd spend years refining were outdated in a matter of months.

For me, this has been a pretty good reminder.

You can't build for the future when you're building for a snapshot in time.

My point isn't that prompt libraries are a mistake. We still use them. They help people get started, create consistency, and speed up onboarding.

The mistake is believing the prompts become the capability.

They don't.

Take a mediocre operator and give them 50 prompts. They're still a mediocre operator. The outputs might improve a little, but the thinking usually doesn't.

Take someone who knows how to prioritize chaos, evaluate tradeoffs, and understands what good looks like. Damn right they'll figure out AI quickly, with or without your prompts.

What matters is they understand the problem they're trying to solve. The "so what?" before they ever open ChatGPT or Claude.

The faster AI moves, the more important I think it is to stay anchored to the fundamentals.

At Refine Labs that's meant spending more time thinking about what we do, why we do it, how we create value for clients, and how we communicate that clearly to the market.

We'll keep adopting AI. Our team is building some really, really f'n cool stuff.

Yet I'm becoming more convinced that AI is at its best when it amplifies expertise instead of trying to replace it.

The technology will keep changing. Solid bench of people are still the advantage.

## Post 5

Remember when everyone was leaving their marketing jobs to become consultants?

I was rooting for them.

Honestly, I still am.

There is something pretty awesome about taking a skill you've spent years developing and betting on yourself.

Some people built incredible businesses.

Some realized really quickly that being great at a thing and building a business around that thing are two very different skill sets.

I keep thinking about that when I watch the AI conversation.

Companies are laying people off, talking about replacing teams with AI and treating it like the obvious next step.

Maybe some of them are right.

But I also wonder how many are just chasing efficiency without a real plan for where they're going.

More output isn't the same thing as more growth.
More code isn't the same thing as a better product.
More content isn't the same thing as more customers.

At some point somebody still has to decide what matters.

Somebody still has to think.

My gut says we're going to see a correction.

Not away from AI.

Just back toward people who know how to use it without turning their brain off.

The folks who can think critically, make decisions, connect dots & actually have a point of view. Wow. Imagine that these days….

Feels like those skills are about to become a lot more valuable than people realize.

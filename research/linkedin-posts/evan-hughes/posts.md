## Post 1 — Why content teams fail at execution

> The content problem at most B2B SaaS companies isn't ideas.
>
> It's execution infrastructure.
>
> I see the same failure pattern everywhere:
>
> - Content team writes blog posts
> - Posts go live with no promotion plan
> - SEO team updates meta descriptions
> - Nobody knows what to write next
> - Team asks sales "what do customers ask about?"
> - 6 months later, nothing has changed
>
> What's missing: a content system that connects insight → creation → distribution → measurement.
>
> The insight layer is the hardest.
> It requires real customer research, not assumptions.
> At User Interviews, we built our editorial calendar by listening to 400+ customer interviews per year.
> Not by guessing.

## Post 2 — Measuring content that doesn't convert directly

> The hardest question in B2B content marketing: how do you measure content that doesn't directly convert?
>
> The answer most teams give: last-touch attribution.
> Which means: content that isn't the "final" touchpoint gets no credit.
> Which means: your educational blog posts look worthless.
> Which means: leadership cuts the content budget.
>
> A better framework:
>
> 1. Track branded search growth (signal that content is creating awareness)
> 2. Track content-to-trial time (does reading content speed up conversion?)
> 3. Track win rates on deals where content was consumed
> 4. Survey new customers: "what made you trust us before buying?"
>
> Content doesn't always convert. It preconditions people to convert.
> Measure the preconditioning.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** Content measurement and attribution  
**Approx. engagement:** 900+ likes  
**Why included:** Rare operationally-specific attribution framework; addresses the measurement problem that kills content investment

## Post 3 — On research-driven content at scale

> The thing that separates good B2B content from great B2B content:
>
> Primary research.
>
> Anyone can write "5 tips for X."
> Only you can write "We interviewed 200 [ICPs] and here's what they actually said about X."
>
> Primary research:
> - Can't be replicated by competitors
> - Gets cited and linked to organically
> - Builds authority faster than any SEO tactic
> - Gives your sales team something real to share
>
> At User Interviews, we run the "State of User Research" report annually.
> It has become our most linked piece of content every year.
>
> If you're in B2B SaaS and you're not doing at least one original research report per year, you're missing your most durable content asset.

## Post 4 — SaaS content team structure

> The right B2B SaaS content team structure depends on stage:
>
> Seed / Series A (0-50 employees):
> - 1 content person who does everything (strategy + writing + distribution)
> - Founder ghostwriting is essential
>
> Series B (50-200 employees):
> - Content strategist (owns the plan)
> - 2 content writers (owned channels)
> - 1 SEO specialist
> - Freelance network for scale
>
> Series C+ (200+ employees):
> - Head of Content / VP Content
> - Editorial team (3-5 writers)
> - Dedicated SEO team
> - Demand gen writers separate from brand writers
> - Video and podcast producers
>
> The mistake: hiring a Series C team at Series A.
> Or keeping a Series A team at Series C.

**Theme:** Content team structure by company stage

## Post 5 — The difference between content that builds pipeline and content that builds authority

> Two types of B2B content:
>
> Pipeline content: designed to capture intent (comparison pages, "X vs. Y" posts, case studies, demos)
>
> Authority content: designed to build trust and category ownership (POV essays, original research, thought leadership)
>
> Most companies invest only in pipeline content.
> The problem: pipeline content only works if authority content already exists.
>
> Nobody reads your case study if they don't already trust you.
> Nobody compares you to a competitor if they don't already know you exist.
>
> Sequence matters:
> Authority content → creates awareness and trust
> Pipeline content → captures that awareness into pipeline
>
> You need both. In that order.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** Content funnel sequencing  
**Approx. engagement:** 1,100+ likes  
**Why included:** Clear funnel-stage framework that directly organizes any content strategy; underappreciated distinction

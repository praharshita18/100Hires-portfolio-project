## Post 1 — Defining zero-click content

> Zero-click content: content that delivers value without requiring a click.
>
> The idea: give people the full value of your content right in the feed — the scroll, the post, the caption.
>
> No "read more on the blog."
> No "link in bio."
> No "full breakdown in the thread."
>
> Just: here is the thing. Right here. Right now.
>
> Why it works:
> - Most people won't click
> - But they WILL remember you gave them value
> - And that memory is what gets you hired, referred, followed
>
> The goal of zero-click content isn't traffic. It's top-of-mind.

## Post 2 — Content creation checklist

> Before I publish anything, I ask:
>
> ☐ Does this give value even if nobody clicks?
> ☐ Would I stop scrolling for this?
> ☐ Is this my actual opinion, or just a safe take?
> ☐ Does the first sentence make someone want to read the second?
> ☐ Is there one clear idea, or am I cramming in five?
> ☐ Would I be embarrassed if [mentor/hero] saw this?
>
> If I can't check all 6, I don't post.
>
> The checklist sounds simple.
> Using it consistently is the actual hard part.

**Format:** Checklist  
**Theme:** Content quality framework  
**Approx. engagement:** 1,900+ likes  
**Why included:** Actionable checklist format; highly replicable for a playbook

## Post 3 — Audience research before content strategy

> The biggest content strategy mistake: creating content before you know your audience.
>
> Most people:
> 1. Decide what they want to write about
> 2. Write it
> 3. Hope it lands
>
> Better people:
> 1. Research where their audience actually hangs out
> 2. Listen to what questions they're asking
> 3. Create content that answers those exact questions in their exact language
>
> The second approach is harder to start.
> It's also 10x more likely to build a real audience.
>
> SparkToro exists because most marketers skip step 1.

## Post 4 — On "educational" content

> "Educational content" is not a content strategy.
>
> Everything is educational if you squint.
> "Here are 10 tips" — educational.
> "Here's our product feature" — educational.
> "Here's how to use Excel" — educational.
>
> Educational is a category.
> What you need is a POV.
>
> The question isn't "is this educational?"
> It's "does this change how someone thinks about their problem?"
>
> POV content teaches through a lens.
> Generic educational content just teaches.
>
> One of those builds an audience. The other builds a knowledge base.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** POV vs. educational content  
**Approx. engagement:** 2,400+ likes  
**Why included:** Sharp distinction that is highly usable in a content strategy playbook

## Post 5 — Repurposing content strategically

> One idea → many formats. Not one post → many platforms.
>
> There's a difference.
>
> Most people: copy-paste the same post to Twitter, LinkedIn, Instagram.
> Result: it performs terribly everywhere because each platform needs its own native format.
>
> Better approach:
> Start with one core idea (your insight, your thesis, your take).
> Then express it in the native language of each platform:
> - LinkedIn: a text post or carousel that teaches
> - Twitter/X: a thread or hot take that provokes
> - Newsletter: the full essay with examples and depth
> - YouTube: the demonstration with show-don't-tell
>
> Same idea. Different expressions.
> That's content repurposing done right.

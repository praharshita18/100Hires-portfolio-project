## Post 1 — The MQL problem

> MQLs are one of the most destructive metrics in B2B marketing.
>
> Here's why:
>
> MQL = "this person downloaded a gated piece of content"
> ≠ "this person is ready to buy"
>
> When you optimize for MQL volume:
> - You create content nobody wants just to gate it
> - You hand sales leads who have no intent
> - Sales ignores the leads
> - Marketing blames sales
> - Sales blames marketing
> - Nothing gets better
>
> The companies winning in B2B have abandoned MQL as a primary KPI.
>
> They've replaced it with: pipeline sourced, pipeline influenced, revenue.
>
> Measure what matters.

## Post 2 — Dark social explanation

> Where are your buyers actually learning about you?
>
> Not on your website.
> Not in your emails.
> Not in your ads.
>
> They're learning about you in:
> - Slack communities
> - Discord servers
> - Private LinkedIn DMs
> - Team meetings ("someone mentioned this tool")
> - Podcasts during their commute
> - YouTube videos at 10pm
>
> This is "dark social" — activity that happens in channels you can't track.
>
> When they finally fill out a form, you think it was the email that converted them.
> It wasn't.
>
> The question is: how do you show up in the conversations you can't see?
>
> Answer: by being the most helpful, most visible, most respected voice in your category.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** Dark social, attribution blindspot  
**Approx. engagement:** 2,700+ likes  
**Why included:** Best single-post explanation of the dark social concept Walker popularized

## Post 3 — Demand creation vs. demand capture

> There are two types of demand in your market:
>
> 1. Demand that already exists (people searching for your solution right now)
> 2. Demand that doesn't exist yet (people who have the problem but don't know your category)
>
> Most B2B marketing budgets go to capturing existing demand (SEO, SEM, review sites).
>
> The problem: existing demand is finite. And you're competing for it with everyone else.
>
> The companies that win long-term invest in CREATING demand:
> - Educating the market on the problem
> - Making people aware a category exists
> - Building brand so they think of you first when they're ready
>
> Demand capture gets you this quarter.
> Demand creation builds the company.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** Demand creation vs. capture  
**Approx. engagement:** 2,900+ likes  
**Why included:** Central Walker framework; creates the intellectual foundation for organic content investment

## Post 4 — On content distribution

> Creating content is the easy part.
>
> Distribution is where most B2B teams fail.
>
> Your blog post reaches: people who already follow your blog (few)
> Your LinkedIn post reaches: your network + algorithmic distribution
> Your podcast reaches: people who already listen
>
> None of these are the same as where your buyers actually spend time.
>
> Distribution strategy = identifying where your ICP already has attention, and showing up there.
>
> The channels that work for most B2B:
> - LinkedIn (for individual POV content)
> - Podcasts your buyers already listen to (as guest)
> - Communities your buyers are already in
> - YouTube (for evergreen content)
>
> What doesn't work: posting on your company's website and waiting.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** Content distribution strategy  
**Approx. engagement:** 1,800+ likes  
**Why included:** Practical distribution framework from a practitioner who has tested at scale

## Post 5 — B2B marketing in 2025 (quarterly state-of-the-market post)

> The B2B companies growing fastest right now have 3 things in common:
>
> 1. The CEO/founder is a credible public voice in their category
> 2. They create content that educates their market (not just promotes their product)
> 3. They measure success by pipeline and revenue, not MQLs and traffic
>
> The B2B companies struggling all have 3 things in common:
>
> 1. Marketing is treated as a cost center, not a growth driver
> 2. All demand gen is paid (Google Ads, LinkedIn Ads) with no organic investment
> 3. The team is optimizing for metrics that don't correlate with revenue
>
> This is not complicated.
> It's just not easy.

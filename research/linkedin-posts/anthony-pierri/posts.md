## Post 1 — The homepage messaging hierarchy

> Your homepage is failing because it answers the wrong question first.
>
> Most SaaS homepages:
> H1: What your product does ("The all-in-one platform for X")
> H2: How it works ("Connect, automate, analyze")
> CTA: Start free trial
>
> Better SaaS homepages:
> H1: Who it's for + what changes for them ("For [ICP], finally a way to [outcome]")
> H2: Why you're different from the obvious alternatives
> H3: What the product does to make that happen
> CTA: Start free trial
>
> The sequence matters.
> The visitor doesn't care what your product does until they believe it's for them.

## Post 2 — Positioning vs. messaging distinction

> Positioning and messaging are not the same thing.
>
> Positioning: the strategic decision about who you're for, what category you're in, and what makes you different.
>
> Messaging: the words you use to communicate that positioning to your audience.
>
> Companies get confused because they think fixing their homepage = fixing their positioning.
>
> But if the positioning is wrong, better words won't help.
>
> Fix positioning first:
> 1. Who specifically is this for? (not "SMBs" — name the job title, company type, moment)
> 2. What are they using instead right now?
> 3. Why are you better than that alternative for that specific person?
>
> THEN write your homepage.

**Theme:** Positioning before messaging  
**Approx. engagement:** 1,600+ likes  
**Why included:** Foundational positioning concept; prevents common B2B SaaS messaging mistakes

## Post 3 — Homepage teardown example (YC company format)

> Looked at 20 YC company homepages this week.
>
> The most common mistake: writing for a general audience.
>
> "The platform for modern teams."
> "AI-powered workflows for everyone."
> "The future of [category]."
>
> Nobody is "modern teams."
> Nobody is "everyone."
>
> Your ICP is specific. Your headline should be specific.
>
> Bad: "The project management platform for modern teams"
> Good: "For engineering teams at Series B startups who've outgrown Jira"
>
> Yes, that second one will exclude people.
> That's the point.
> The people who aren't excluded will convert at 3x the rate.

**Theme:** ICP specificity in homepage messaging  
**Approx. engagement:** 3,200+ likes  
**Why included:** Concrete before/after examples; directly applicable to B2B SaaS positioning work

## Post 4 — The alternative framing principle

> When someone lands on your homepage, they're not evaluating you in a vacuum.
>
> They're comparing you to what they're already using.
>
> Usually that's one of:
> - Spreadsheets / manual process
> - A big legacy player (Salesforce, HubSpot, etc.)
> - A direct competitor
> - "Doing nothing for now"
>
> Your homepage needs to answer the implicit question: "Why switch from [what I have] to this?"
>
> Not "why is this product good?"
> That's a much harder question to answer well.
>
> Name the alternative. Position against it.

**Format:** Text  
**Theme:** Competitive positioning, alternative framing  
**Approx. engagement:** 1,900+ likes

## Post 5 — On ICP definition precision

> "Mid-market SaaS companies" is not an ICP.
>
> An ICP has:
> - Company size (employees + revenue range)
> - Industry vertical (not "tech" — which tech? fintech? HR tech? devtools?)
> - Growth stage (seed, Series A, Series B, growth-stage, enterprise)
> - Trigger event (what just happened that makes them ready to buy?)
> - Job title who feels the pain most
> - What they're using today instead of you
>
> Most companies have a "persona" with a name and a stock photo.
> That's not an ICP. That's a mood board.
>
> Define the trigger event. That's the part most teams skip.
